Talent: Press Interviews -- Have a Conversation, Not a Message

Commentary

Summary

Many believe press interviews should be scripted marketing speeches. Jonathan rejected this, arguing that a successful interview should be a conversation with insight, not regurgitated marketing messages. A good communications person understands the difference between messaging and conversation. Messages don't answer questions; conversations answer intelligently with insights and stories while reinforcing the message without parroting it. As communications colleague Ellen West says, "To be a thought leader, you have to have a thought."

Key Patterns

In an age of transparency, scripted responses are easily detected and undermine credibility. Having genuine conversations with journalists requires more skill and courage than reciting talking points, but produces far more effective results. The insight that "if you aren't being criticized, you probably didn't have a good conversation" reveals that real engagement involves productive tension. The world can spot messaging a mile away.

Practical Applications

Replace scripted FAQs with genuine preparation that develops real insights. Practice having conversations, not delivering messages. Accept that authentic press engagement will sometimes result in criticism. Push your team to discover insights rather than polish messages.
