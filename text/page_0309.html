Commentary

Summary

Endnote 147 attributes a widely-quoted saying to Elmore Leonard, the master of hard-boiled crime fiction who died at 87 in 2013, as reported in the Los Angeles Times obituary by Dennis McLellan. Leonard was famous for his writing advice about leaving out the parts readers tend to skip.

Key Patterns

The authors' reference to a fiction writer's advice about concise communication reflects a recurring theme: great communicators in any field -- whether novelists, executives, or engineers -- understand the value of brevity and cutting unnecessary content. This applies directly to the Google culture of clear, focused communication.

Practical Applications

Apply Elmore Leonard's principle to business communication: leave out the parts people tend to skip. Whether writing emails, creating presentations, or running meetings, ruthlessly edit for clarity and conciseness. Respect your audience's time and attention by focusing only on what truly matters.
