Eric's Notes for a Strategy Meeting

Commentary

Summary

The chapter on Culture concludes with a Nietzsche quote about being proud of competitors while not following them. Then Eric shares his collected strategy meeting wisdom -- pearls gathered from whiteboards, Post-its, and emails over years of strategy sessions. Key insights include: the right strategy has a beauty to it with many people and ideas working in concert; start by asking what will be true in five years and work backward; examine factors where technology is exponentially driving down cost curves; identify disrupters and opportunities; and win on product and platform since market information is now nearly perfect.

Key Patterns

The "work backward from the future" approach to strategy is a powerful technique used by many successful companies, notably Amazon. The emphasis on product and platform over traditional competitive advantages reflects the Internet Century reality that information asymmetry no longer provides sustainable advantages. The two-scenario framework for disruption (incumbent vs. challenger) provides a practical mental model for strategic thinking. The advice to consider aligned incentives from outside players shows sophisticated game-theory thinking applied to business strategy.

Practical Applications

When planning strategy sessions, focus the first exercise on identifying what will be true in five years, especially regarding technology cost curves and emerging platforms. Spend the vast majority of time on product and platform discussions rather than competitive analysis. When facing disruption as an incumbent, develop visceral understanding of the technical insights the challenger will use. As a challenger, map out the tools incumbents will use to stop you (business relationships, regulations, lawsuits). Always include people outside your existing framework who can think about innovation alongside insiders.
