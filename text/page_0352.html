Endnotes -- Sunk Costs and Escalation of Commitment

Commentary

Summary

This endnote (190) discusses how the desire to recoup sunk costs leads people to stick with bad courses of action and even double down, a pattern scholars call "escalation of commitment." The note observes that from an individual's perspective, investing based on sunk costs can be rational since it protects the decision-maker's reputation within the organization. It references key academic works by Barry M. Staw and by McAfee, Mialon, and Mialon.

Key Patterns

The sunk cost fallacy is one of the most pervasive cognitive biases in business. The endnote makes a subtle but important point: escalation of commitment can be individually rational even when it is organizationally destructive, because admitting a project is doomed can harm the decision-maker's career. This creates a systemic problem that requires cultural solutions -- organizations must make it safe to kill failing projects.

Practical Applications

Create organizational cultures where killing a failing project is celebrated as good judgment rather than punished as failure. Separate the decision to continue a project from the reputation of the person who initiated it. Use objective metrics and regular reviews to evaluate projects independently of how much has already been invested.
