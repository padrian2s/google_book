Talent: Relationships and Praise

Commentary

Summary

Building relationships requires noting the little things -- partner's and kids' names, important family issues -- which can be easily recorded in your contacts. Eric believes in the three-week rule for new positions: listen to people, understand their issues and priorities, get to know and care about them, and earn their trust. This is actually doing something important: establishing a healthy relationship. And praise is underused and underappreciated as a management tool. When it is deserved, don't hold back.

Key Patterns

The emphasis on personal details and genuine care stands in contrast to purely transactional professional relationships. The three-week listening period demonstrates that effective leadership often requires patience rather than immediate action. The closing note on praise is significant -- in performance-driven cultures, recognition can be overlooked in favor of constant improvement, but genuine praise reinforces the behaviors and relationships that drive success.

Practical Applications

Record personal details about colleagues in your contacts system. When starting a new role, dedicate the first three weeks to listening and relationship-building. Make praise a regular management practice -- specific, timely, and genuine recognition strengthens both relationships and performance culture.
