Culture: Keep Them Crowded

Commentary

Summary

While Google's amenities (volleyball courts, gourmet cafeterias, espresso machines) impress visitors, the real cultural foundation lies in the crowded, messy offices where employees actually work. The authors challenge the traditional office layout where space and quiet signify status. At Google, offices are designed to maximize energy and interactions, not isolation and status. Smart creatives thrive on interaction -- cramming them together creates combustible mixtures of ideas. The steady state should be highly interactive and boisterous, not quiet. Private retreats (nap pods, small conference rooms, outdoor spaces) exist for when people need a break from stimulation, but the default is collaborative crowding. This approach also kills facilities envy -- when no one has a private office, no one complains.

Key Patterns

Physical space as a manifestation of cultural values -- egalitarian culture requires egalitarian space. The inversion of traditional office hierarchy where space equals status. The design principle of making interaction the default state rather than an exception (meetings). The Silicon Valley tradition of generous amenities dating back to HP in the 1960s, serving the purpose of keeping people at the office, not as luxury. The insight that serendipitous encounters drive innovation more than planned meetings.

Practical Applications

Design offices to maximize interaction and energy rather than privacy and quiet. Eliminate status-based office allocation -- the CEO shouldn't have the biggest office. Provide retreat spaces for when people need quiet, but make the default state collaborative. Invest heavily in the resources people need to do their work (computing power, tools) while being stingy with status symbols (corner offices, fancy furniture).
