Culture: Establish a Culture of Yes

Commentary

Summary

The "Just Say No" syndrome can kill smart creative cultures. Companies develop elaborate processes, approvals, and meetings that are all passive-aggressive ways to say no. Enough no's and smart creatives stop asking and head for the exits. The authors advocate establishing a culture of Yes -- delaying new processes as long as possible and setting a high bar for creating approval gates. YouTube's Salar Kamangar demonstrated this when his team had a feature (high-definition playback) testing well: rather than following the schedule that said to wait weeks, he asked if there was any real reason not to launch immediately. There wasn't, so HD YouTube launched the next day. Millions of users benefited weeks early from one leader's commitment to saying yes.

Key Patterns

Process accumulation as organizational entropy -- each new approval gate is a small "no" that compounds over time. The reflexive no as a signal that a company has lost its start-up verve. Smart creatives as canaries in the coal mine -- when they stop asking and start leaving, the culture of no has won. The power of asking "is there any real reason not to?" instead of "are all the boxes checked?" The distinction between necessary process (helping the company scale) and unnecessary process (institutional inertia).

Practical Applications

Audit your processes and approval gates regularly -- each one should have a compelling business justification. When something is ready and working, ask "is there any real reason not to launch?" rather than defaulting to the schedule. Delay creating new processes as long as possible. Set a high bar for adding any new approval step -- the cost of saying no is often invisible but cumulative.
