Expand the Aperture

Commentary

Summary

The ideal candidate has passion, intellect, integrity, and a unique perspective. Finding and hiring her requires four links: sourcing, interviewing, hiring, and compensation. Sourcing starts with defining the candidate type, and Google's recruiting partner Martha Josephson calls this "expanding the aperture" -- like a camera lens, a wider aperture captures more. A typical hiring manager has a narrow aperture, considering only people with certain titles in certain fields. The successful manager sets a wider aperture. If you want talent from a specific company that guards its people, expand the aperture to find those who want new challenges their current employer can't provide -- the engineer blocked from moving to product management, the PM who wants sales experience. People willing to take risks introduce the exact self-selection tendency you want. Hire the best engineer regardless of coding language preference.

Key Patterns

The camera aperture metaphor brilliantly captures the trade-off in hiring: a narrow aperture gives predictable but limited results, while a wider aperture captures unexpected brilliance. The self-selection insight is powerful -- people willing to take career risks to join you are demonstrating exactly the risk-tolerant, growth-oriented mindset you want. The Kevin Systrom story (denied transfer to APM program because he lacked a CS degree, left to cofound Instagram) is a cautionary tale about aperture too narrow even for Google. Supercomputer pioneer Seymour Cray deliberately hired for inexperience to get people who "do not usually know what's supposed to be impossible."

Practical Applications

When sourcing candidates, deliberately look beyond the obvious profile. Consider people from adjacent roles, industries, or backgrounds who show trajectory and learning ability. If a candidate has the right fundamentals but lacks specific experience, that inexperience may actually be an advantage -- they won't be constrained by "how it's always been done." Give smart recent graduates vital roles on real projects despite their inexperience. Judge candidates on trajectory, not just current position.
