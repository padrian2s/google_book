Culture: fun, not Fun

Commentary

Summary

The authors distinguish between Fun (manufactured corporate events with forced activities) and fun (organic enjoyment that arises naturally from the culture). Most company picnics, holiday parties, and off-sites with "team-building" exercises aren't actually fun. The formula for a fun event is simple: great people + great music + great food and drink. For off-sites, forget "team building" and focus on genuinely enjoyable experiences people couldn't have on their own. Examples include trips to Muir Woods, elephant seal watching, and the Santa Cruz Boardwalk. Fun can be cheap -- Larry and Sergey's roller hockey games cost nothing but a stick and willingness to be hip-checked by a founder. Sergey's humor at TGIF meetings, Eric dancing "Gangnam Style" with PSY in Seoul, Jonathan washing a mud-caked stretch Hummer after losing a bet -- these moments of genuine fun drive culture more than any planned Fun event.

Key Patterns

The lowercase-f versus uppercase-F distinction: organic fun versus manufactured Fun. Leaders participating in fun (dancing poorly, losing bets) rather than organizing it from above. The insight that fun culture and innovative culture are fundamentally the same -- both require a permissive environment. Fun as a natural by-product of great people enjoying each other's company, not as an HR initiative. The principle that leaders should set broad boundaries for what's permissible rather than prescribing fun activities.

Practical Applications

Stop manufacturing Fun and start creating conditions for organic fun. When planning events, focus on great people, great food, and great experiences rather than structured team-building exercises. As a leader, participate in fun rather than organizing it -- dance poorly, take bets, be willing to be the butt of jokes. Keep the boundaries of permissible behavior as broad as possible to encourage organic creativity and humor.
