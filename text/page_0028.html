Culture: Don't Listen to the HiPPOs

Commentary

Summary

HiPPOs -- the Highest-Paid Person's Opinion -- are dangerous in companies. In "tenurocracies," power derives from tenure, not merit. Creating a meritocracy requires both the hippo (who could rule by fiat) and the brave smart creative (who risks getting trampled). The authors tell of Sridhar Ramaswamy, who disagreed with Sergey Brin's idea for the AdWords platform. Despite being the highest-paid person, Sergey didn't simply order compliance. After much debate, Sergey's idea was discarded. This worked because Sergey understood the data, technology, and context deeply enough to engage on merits, and had the self-confidence to cede control when his idea wasn't best. For meritocracies to function, there must be an "obligation to dissent" -- if someone thinks an idea is wrong, they must raise it or be culpable for the outcome.

Key Patterns

The HiPPO problem: pay level and tenure becoming proxies for decision-making authority regardless of idea quality. The "obligation to dissent" as a cultural norm -- dissent must be required, not just permitted. The two-sided requirement for meritocracy: leaders must be willing to lose arguments, and employees must be willing to challenge leaders. The connection between deep understanding and willingness to cede control -- leaders who understand the details are more comfortable being overruled.

Practical Applications

Establish dissent as an obligation, not just a right -- silence equals complicity. As a leader, demonstrate willingness to lose arguments when the data or reasoning is against you. Make decisions based on data and argument quality, not the seniority of the person presenting. Build enough technical understanding to engage with your team's work on its merits, which paradoxically makes it easier to step back when someone else has a better idea.
