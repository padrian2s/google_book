Commentary

Summary

The glossary continues with definitions of key terms: learning animals (people who love learning and handle massive change), Memegen (internal meme creation site), Moma (Google's intranet), Moore's Law (computing power doubles every two years), multisided market, Noogler (new Google employee), obligation to dissent (must raise concerns), OKR (Objectives and Key Results), open (sharing IP and using open standards), payback period, platform, ROI, scale, smart creative (a person combining deep technical knowledge with intelligence, business savvy, and creative qualities), tenurocracies, TGIF, user interface, Wave, and Web 2.0.

Key Patterns

The glossary captures Google's distinctive organizational language. Terms like "obligation to dissent" codify cultural expectations into memorable phrases. "Smart creative" provides a label for the type of employee the entire book is about -- someone who combines technical depth with business acumen and creativity. The definition of "open" is notably specific, encompassing not just open-source code but open standards and giving customers freedom to exit your platform.

Practical Applications

Adopt the "obligation to dissent" principle in your organization -- make it a cultural expectation that people must speak up when they see problems. Define what "smart creative" means for your industry and hire accordingly. Create your own organizational vocabulary that encodes your cultural values into memorable, shareable terms.
