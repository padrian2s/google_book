Career -- Choose the F-16

Commentary

Summary

The authors shift to career advice, offering wisdom for budding entrepreneurs, new Googlers, and emerging superstars. The first insight: Treat your career like surfing. When Jonathan was in business school, a consumer packaged goods company described product management as "driving forward by looking in your rearview mirror" -- data-driven but backward-looking. Then a Silicon Valley firm described product management as "flying an F-16 at Mach 2 over a boulder-strewn landscape, two meters off the ground" with plenty of quarters to crash and restart. The best industries are the F-16 ones. In business, you need to catch at least one big wave and ride it to shore. People right out of school tend to prioritize company first, then job, then industry -- but this is exactly the wrong order. The right industry is paramount because switching industries is much harder than switching companies.

Key Patterns

The surfing metaphor for career management captures the importance of being in the right place (industry) and catching the right wave (company). The F-16 vs. rearview mirror contrast encapsulates the difference between dynamic and static industries. The counterintuitive industry-first advice challenges the common career wisdom that focuses on finding the right company or role. The reasoning is sound: industries determine the available opportunity set, and you'll switch companies multiple times anyway. The most interesting industries are those where product cycle times are accelerating, creating disruption opportunities.

Practical Applications

When making career decisions, prioritize industry over company over job title. Choose industries where technology is driving rapid change and creating new opportunities. From a compensation perspective, early career equity is limited so industry expertise is more valuable; later, as equity packages grow, the specific company wave matters more. Don't fear being in the wrong company within the right industry -- the skills and relationships transfer. If you're in a stagnant industry early in your career, consider making a move before your industry expertise locks you in.
