Introduction: The Call for a Business Plan

Commentary

Summary

Board member Moritz's email was a call to action for a comprehensive business plan covering product, sales, marketing, finance, and corporate development. There was even talk of restructuring Google from its quirky start-up model into traditional business units. Since products were the crux of the plan, Eric assigned the project to Jonathan Rosenberg. But the authors recognized a fundamental problem: while a traditional plan would satisfy the board, it would conflict with how Google actually operated. The chapter introduces the founding philosophy of "just go talk to the engineers" -- Larry and Sergey ran the company by hiring brilliant engineers and giving them freedom, using communication and informal meetings rather than formal planning structures.

Key Patterns

The tension between traditional corporate governance (board expectations for formal business plans) and innovation-driven culture. The founders' deliberate choice to run the company like an academic lab rather than a traditional corporation. The "top 100 projects" spreadsheet as a lightweight management tool versus elaborate planning frameworks. The pattern of hiring for technical excellence first, with business acumen as secondary.

Practical Applications

Resist the pressure to adopt traditional business planning frameworks when they conflict with what actually makes your company successful. Focus on hiring exceptional people and giving them freedom rather than creating elaborate management processes. Use lightweight, transparent tools for resource allocation rather than complex top-down planning. When stakeholders demand formal plans, find ways to satisfy their needs without undermining your operational culture.
