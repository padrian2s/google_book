Strategy: Don't Follow Competition

Commentary

Summary

Business leaders are obsessed with competition, but this fixation leads to a spiral into mediocrity. Like match-race sailing where the lead boat tacks whenever the follower does, incumbents clump together so no one finds fresher wind elsewhere. Larry Page asks: how exciting is it to come to work if the best you can do is trounce a similar competitor? Focusing on competition means never delivering anything truly innovative. While you fight over market share fractions, someone who doesn't care will build a new platform that changes the game. But competition isn't irrelevant -- it keeps you sharp. When Microsoft launched Bing in 2009, Google's all-hands response led to innovations like Google Instant and Image Search. Nietzsche wrote: "You must be proud of your enemy; then your enemy's successes are also your successes." Be proud of your competitors, just don't follow them.

Key Patterns

Competitive obsession as a path to mediocrity -- following competitors means never leading. The sailing metaphor: covering tactics keep you close but prevent finding stronger wind. The paradox that competition motivates improvement without deserving to be the focus. Larry Page's reframe: the job is to think of what hasn't been thought of yet, not to beat competitors at what they already do. The productive use of competitive threats (Bing inspiring Google Instant) versus the unproductive fixation on matching competitor features.

Practical Applications

Use competition as motivation but not as your strategic compass. Focus on what customers need but haven't imagined yet rather than matching competitor features. When a competitor launches something threatening, use it as fuel for innovation rather than as a template to copy. Reframe competitive discussions: instead of "how do we beat them at X?" ask "what can we build that makes X irrelevant?"
